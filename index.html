Historical Analysis

William Wordsworth – I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud

Historical Analysis

William Wordsworth's poem "I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud" was written in 1804 and first published in 1807, during a period of significant political and cultural upheaval in Europe. Although the poem itself is a quiet reflection of nature, it emerged during the late 18th and early 19th centuries, which were drastically shaped by the chaos of the French Revolution and the rise of Romanticism.

In 1787, he entered college, and it was during those coming-of-age years that the French Revolution, one of the most influential transformations in European history, occurred in 1789.

Wordsworth first traveled to France on a walking tour in 1790, arriving during the Revolution’s hopeful, idealistic phase. During this period, he was immediately drawn to the Revolution’s Enlightenment ideals of liberty, equality, and the challenge to centuries of monarchy. This blend of political excitement and the natural landscapes of France and the Alps had a significant influence on his poetry and political beliefs.

However, as the Revolution descended into violence during the Reign of Terror, Wordsworth became deeply disillusioned with revolutionary ideals. The Reign of Terror was backed by the working poor against aristocratic counterrevolutionaries, which offended Wordsworth’s middle-class ideals and need for reform. He found the ensuing violence and extremism shocking and ultimately viewed the Revolution as a move from “respectable” beginnings to “wickedness.”

In his Letter to the Bishop of Llandaff, he objects to the violence and bloodshed of the French Revolution, writing: “They [thinking and feeling men] are sorry that the prejudice and weakness of mankind have made it necessary to force an individual into an unnatural situation, which requires more than human talents and human virtues.” This passage shows Wordsworth’s disillusionment with the Revolution’s extremism and his concern for individuals trapped by circumstances beyond their control. This collapse of his hopes turned him away from politics and into themes of memory, imagination, and emotion. Nature became a form of refuge for Wordsworth.

By the time he wrote “I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud,” Europe was again in turmoil under Napoleon, and Wordsworth’s belief in nature became his escape to heal and stabilize.

This historical period also marked the rise of Romanticism, which rejected Enlightenment rationalism and emphasized emotion, imagination, and the natural world. As Wordsworth rejected the French Revolution, he also rejected Enlightenment ideals and instead embraced Romantic ideals, becoming a central figure in defining Romanticism—especially through his devotion to ordinary subjects, his connection to nature, and his personal emotional experience.

“I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud” embodies these principles by focusing not on political events but on the personal encounter between the writer and the natural world.

When Wordsworth’s collection Poems, in Two Volumes (1807), which included “I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud,” was first published, it was met with harsh criticism. The most influential critic of the Romantic era was Francis Jeffery, who used the Edinburgh Review to attack Wordsworth’s poetic language.

In 1807, Jeffery directly targeted Wordsworth, opening his review with: “If the printing of such trash as this be not felt as an insult on the public taste, we are afraid it cannot be insulted” (The Critical Reception 1807–1818). Jeffery’s main complaint was that Wordsworth wrote “upon principle and system,” arguing that Wordsworth’s attempt to use ordinary subjects and language resulted not in authenticity, but in forced simplicity.

This early reception positioned Wordsworth as a controversial figure. Yet, at the same time, others began to recognize the originality and emotional depth of his work. Ironically, the Romantic qualities—simplicity, emotional sincerity, and the spiritual power of nature—that Jeffery condemned would later become core Romantic values, inspiring generations of poets to value ordinary life and the beauty of the natural world.
